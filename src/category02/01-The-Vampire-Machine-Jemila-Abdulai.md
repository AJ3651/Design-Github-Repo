# The Vampire Machine:  AI in the creation of art

## Jemila Abdulai 

**Why AI art does not sit well with me**

<div class="body-text">

I vaguely remember the first time I came across an artwork made with AI. It was a short film with impressive graphics. It had vibrant and captivating moving images that morphed from one scene to the next. I was in awe.  I began to feel the shared sense of excitement in the air at the time, excited for the possibilities for making my own short films.   
As someone who at the time never thought I could ever make these impressive and technically complex artworks myself, AI seemed like the perfect avenue for me to get closer to a world I had always wanted to be a part of. 

Now about two years later, I have moved quite far onto the opposite side of the spectrum about the use of AI models in art. In fact, I often find myself feeling a negative reaction I can't quite articulate properly yet, when I come across these same kinds of artworks browsing online or on social media today. 

The term ‘AI’ (Artificial Intelligence) has been used to describe ‘*the capability of computational systems to perform tasks typically associated with human intelligence, such as learning, reasoning, problem-solving, perception, and decision-making.*’ However in recent years, due to the rise of Generative AI models such as ChatGPT and Midjourney, AI is known for a different purpose- making art.

Upon its initial introduction to the masses, Generative AI models seemed to be the future. The newest breakthrough in human technology, the ‘inevitable’ turn in human history that was supposed to fix all our problems.   
Suddenly it was everywhere, and as someone who was always more of a techno-optimist, I thought this was a great turn of events. 

All too soon however, AI was *everywhere* – and not in a good way.   
Seemingly, every new tech startup launched was now an AI startup; and established companies that typically had nothing to do with technology were suddenly making ‘AI-driven strategy’ pivots; almost like they were trying to wedge AI assistants into any part of their business model they could. 

On social media, there were many more ads pushing all the ways this shiny new technology made life so much better; with some even advertising a frankly dystopian product called ‘Friend’. This is a pendant embedded with an AI ‘companion’ you could wear around your neck and served as a partner to converse with day-to-day. Needless to say, it was not very well received by a lot of people, judging from the vandalism and graffiti it received on its ads around the city of New York. 

Even in my personal life, the name ‘ChatGPT’ was everywhere, with people starting to refer to the LLM using anthropomorphised terms like ‘He/She’. Even more alarmingly, it was becoming more normalised to ask it for advice about things like where to visit on holiday, what to eat for dinner or even what to do about the issues you were having with a friend. With this in mind, I started to realise how embedded it was becoming in our day to day lives; and often not by choice. 

Within the world of art, the ‘AI aesthetic’ emerged and along with it came the realisation for many artists that their works were being stolen and used to train these models to produce content without their consent. 

In light of this, the wave of anti-AI sentiment became even more visible within the creative spaces I navigated. It was more common to label someone as ‘not a real artist’ if they had used AI in creating an artwork. Even the mention of AI in a creative context was regularly met with hostility and disdain.  
   
The topic has become something very polarising today, with still no clear consensus about the legality of using models that have been trained with stolen or copyrighted artworks to create art. Without a doubt however, it’s safe to say the impact of AI on artists has been quite profound.  

The loss of intellectual property is another outcome of the AI boom that must be discussed. Due to the aforementioned lack of legal clarity of using AI to produce art works, notions of authorship are often unclear and debatable. The referenced paper ‘The GPT Judge’  further explores this topic in further detail.

Consequently, the phenomenon of cognitive offloading is another aspect of the AI boom that is increasingly becoming more and more evident.  
   
The outcome is clear \- AI is making us lazier; according to a paper published by Fayaz et al. For some artists, using AI sometimes means offloading the essential tasks of art making such as ideation and execution to these LLMs in the name of efficiency and optimisation. 

It is my personal belief that the most important parts of being an artist are the ability to be able to develop ideas in a way that feels subversive and meaningful; by being able to question and break norms and potentially collaborate with others to do so. Art has largely been used as a vehicle for change in our society.  

However it seems increasingly, some relatively renowned artists do not resonate with that, which to me was unexpected. 

Upon listening to a talk by the artist called ‘niceaunties’ who almost predominantly uses AI tools to make their work, something seemed amiss to me. Hearing them talk about how much nicer it is to work with AI because it *‘doesn't get angry when you ask for changes’* or how *‘the line of production has shrunk and therefore become more efficient*’ is something that feels out of place for a practice that seeks to challenge, explore and question things. 

The artist here in question describes their own practice as intended to *‘rewrite the stories of women*’ making them *‘unapologetically free’*.  However, it seemed ironic to me to use a generative AI tool like KlingAI or Seedance to ‘rewrite stories’ when a major problem with AI is that it is regressive in nature.

AI models are often trained to operate on probability \- meaning they operate by comparing what *‘is’* to what has already been. Google itself mentions using the Bayesian model as part of training its models, leading us to believe that common AI models are trained to always bring the output back to what exists and what has been done before. The outcome of this current model is that a lot of art ends up looking the same, because these models are drawing from the same probability distributions and end up simply producing the ‘average sum’ of a piece of artwork, based on all of the ones that exist in its dataset. 

In my opinion, this makes it quite static in nature. It easily becomes a restricting force for an artist wanting to try something new, because it is so heavily based on reading information from previously gathered datasets.   
AI is not easily able to create new arguments for norms to be rewritten and deviated from. When artists use it, they often cannot break out from the norm and question things, which arguably is one of the core purposes for art creation. 

A lot of modern artists increasingly rely on technology or even ‘non-human’ collaborators to produce ideas and make art which is not a problem in itself. As a computational artist myself, I believe combining art and technology is a wonderful way to allow artists to express themselves in ways they may have not been able to do before, and be more audacious with their work.  
Jenna Sutela’s work ‘nimiia cétiï’ is a brilliant and imaginative example of this. Her work uses machine learning, neural networks and bacteria to create a narrative that is rich and unique. 

On the other hand however, when the artist becomes increasingly detached from the ideation, execution and implementation of an artwork, one begins to wonder whether the outcome is truly something we can consider to be ‘art’. 

From my point of view, we must first start by asking *‘what is art and is AI art actually art?’ Who is art for? Why does it need to be optimised?’.*

Additionally, we must question what purpose the creation of art serves; is it solely for generating income or for questioning the norm and imagining the world in a different way?

In my opinion, art is a way to express our thoughts and emotions, to communicate, and to connect with one another on the basis of our shared human experience. It doesn't seem right as an artist to create your own echo chamber of thoughts and narratives, completely disconnected from the reality of day to day life; devoid of the quality of human experience, just because it’s ‘more efficient’.  

I strongly believe in the idea that there is no efficiency in creation.   
In an ideal world, there should be very little place for trying to be efficient in the creative process because efficiency and producing should not be the goal. The toil of ideation and creation for an artist is a worthwhile process that should not be fast-tracked. If we *‘try to do everything efficiently, we are turning off the power of serendipity*’. As Dimacali mentions in their article on AI Art *‘just because we can, doesn't mean we should*’. 

AI is further perpetuating the capitalist mindset within art spaces that the purpose of creating art is for the market, or that production and scale are the end goal. The effect of this way of thinking on art and artists tries to rationalise it as a matter of fact.   
Despite this, we need to keep reminding ourselves about the purpose that art is intended to serve, before allowing this mentality to further creep into spaces where they are actively causing damage.

Indeed, the impact of AI is a serious societal and needless to mention \- environmental issue. Despite this, we must all tackle it with a shared vision to protect the rights of artists and the general developmental and intellectual well-being of our society. 

 All things considered, the first major steps we need to take as a society are to create regulations to protect artists and put safety measures in place that actually have the best interest of the wider community at the center. Leaving these decisions at the hands of the CEOs of big tech companies who seem to have less and less interest in being humane and socially conscious is a guaranteed way for things to spin even more out of control. 

Before we accept using AI in the artistic creation process, AI models as they are need a massive overhaul. No more should they be these massive black holes of data, consuming any form of input to the extent of even cannibalising themselves. Perhaps it might be better to create smaller, more intentional models whose data sets have been more ethically produced and gathered. Models created for a specific purpose, either by the artists themselves or in collaboration with others in their network.   
The purpose of technology should not be trying to ‘solve’ art and make it repeatable or scalable; it should be to provide artists with a way of expressing themselves that they didn't have before.

Although AI currently seems to be advancing at a speed that feels overwhelming to imagine attempting to control and regulate, we all deserve to live, work and create in spaces that intrinsically support, rather than detract from our creativity and expression; and therefore our human experience itself.
