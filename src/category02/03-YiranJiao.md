| **An Empty Judgment Produced by Technology**  |  |

Imagine that one day, you are told that an algorithmic risk system has placed you on a watchlist. Your past records, your place of residence, your social relations: this set of quantifiable features is close enough to those of a certain high-risk group; and so, even though you may not have committed any illegal act, you still receive a determination about yourself: a score, a risk level, contact from the police. This system, Chicago's "Strategic Subject List," ran for several years before it was shut down.¹ So what I want to ask is: why can an operational result about an individual be produced before that individual's concrete action has even taken place, and change the way he is treated?

¹ The "Strategic Subject List" is taken here as an example because it is a closed case that has already run its full course and can be looked back on; but this logic did not end when it was shut down. Similar predictive tracking, targeting, and risk-determination systems are still widely deployed today across different contexts. What this essay is concerned with is their shared structure, not the specific circumstances of any single deployment.

1. Judgment

To see clearly what this system does to judgment, we must first describe the old structure of judgment. Following Mireille Hildebrandt's account of the "informational" character of law, modern legal judgment is organized around actions that have already taken place, not around some persistent state of the subject. Concrete action is the object of judgment: a person can anticipate in advance that signing a contract and then failing to perform it may bring liability, precisely because the connection between "action and consequence" lets the meaning of a behaviour be grasped before it unfolds and confirmed after it unfolds. Reasons are the mediation through which an action can be interpreted; a transfer of money may be fraud, may be support of a dependent, may be repayment of a debt, and the same external behaviour points to different structures of responsibility under different reasons. Context is the background through which action and reasons can be read. These three together support the whole structure.

At the same time, Hildebrandt adds a feature that is crucial to what follows: judgment can be modified. It is not a result that forms once and is thereby closed, but a process continually reopened by new actions, facts, and explanations. The subject's later behaviour flows back, and can loosen the initial handling. Even an everyday prejudgment based on accent or clothing is no more neutral than an algorithmic score; but it takes place within a concrete scene, and a person can resist it with action. This capacity to be reopened is precisely what keeps judgment open to the correction of itself.

2. "Judgment"

Now return to the "Strategic Subject List." Its operational logic is to infer future risk from past data. A person's records, location, risk of victimisation, and social relations are entered into the model, in order to calculate the possibility that he will become a shooting victim or perpetrator in the future. The person placed on the list may never have carried out any violent act; he may not even have done anything in the present scene that needs to be examined. He enters the system only because his data position is close enough to that of a certain high-risk group. What Antoinette Rouvroy and Thomas Berns call the "profile" comes into play here: what the system makes contact with is a statistical position recomposed from digital traces and everyday trajectories. These materials strictly belong to the past, and often do not belong to the subject's own actions: a high score may originate from the neighbourhood he lived in a few years ago, or from several records of co-appearing with a marked person. It is thin in attribution. What it records is a network position. What the subject did as an agent barely enters the calculation. And so the three conditions are absent one by one. Concrete action disappears: the basis for handling this person is a calculated closeness to future risk, and has nothing to do with what he did. Reasons disappear: what the model gives is correlation, a relation of co-occurrence between data points, and it cannot be refuted or corrected the way an interpretation of reasons can. Context disappears, or rather is displaced: the background is past data compressed into features, and it often belongs to a group: the crime rate of a neighbourhood, the statistical profile of a category of population, while the consequences fall on the individual.

And that capacity to "be reopened" is also absorbed in reverse. If a person marked as high-risk later carries out no violence, this does not overturn the handling of him. It can be read as the risk having never existed, or as the risk having already been preemptively intervened in, and either way, the score is preserved. The subject's later behaviour, his appeals, and even the fact that "the predicted behaviour never happened" are all absorbed as inputs into the model's next round of self-optimisation. What once could reopen judgment is converted into material that makes the system more precise and therefore more impossible to hold accountable. The channel of correction becomes a device that reinforces the initial handling.

This is the structure I want to call "empty judgment." Judgment is still operating, complete and efficient, with classification, contact, intervention, and consequences all falling on the subject. What has been hollowed out are the conditions on which judgment once stood: the action that was its object, the reasons that were its mediation, the context that let it be read, and the openness that kept it modifiable. The operation continues; the ground beneath its feet is no longer there. The occurrence of judgment no longer passes through the subject, no longer needs the subject's building and repair; instead, an external eye examines and measures the distance between the subject and each of the standards.

What technological mediation removes is not arbitrary. The conditions that are stripped away: concrete action, present context, the backflow of correction—are precisely those that depend on a span of time that has not yet arrived, that depend on an event still waiting to unfold. They cannot be computed in advance, because they have not yet happened. What the system bypasses is exactly these, while letting the part that can be computed in advance bear, on its own, the operational weight of the whole event. The SSL is only the clearest example of this; the same process applies to any system that has a high degree of operational access to the subject: credit scoring, recidivism prediction, risk profiling, all alike. What is finally left is a judgment that still falls fully on the person, while the conditions that once made it a judgment rather than a label imposed from outside have been bypassed.

And these bypassed conditions are not merely the technical parts of judgment. Concrete action, statable reasons, correction that can flow back. They are precisely the qualification by which a person is treated as a subject who can argue back, can respond, can be understood anew. When judgment can take effect as usual while bypassing them, what is suspended is not only certain conditions of judgment, but the subject's position to speak within this judgment.

Together, the two projects position recognition and opacity not as simple opposites, but as two conditions within the same encounter between machine vision and non-human life. Recognition can interrupt indifference and bring an overlooked life into attention. Yet complete legibility may also reduce that life to the categories through which a system is able to recognise it.

More powerful hardware, faster cameras, larger datasets or different detection models may eventually reduce, or even remove, many of the failures observed in the project. The significance of these failures therefore does not depend on their permanence. During this brief interval, the pigeon remains within the camera’s field of view, yet the model can no longer hold its moving body within a stable computational form. As the wing folds, accelerates and changes its outline, recognition is momentarily suspended. The bird does not leave the technological system. Instead, its movement opens a fold within recognition itself: a moment in which it remains present, but not fully resolved.
