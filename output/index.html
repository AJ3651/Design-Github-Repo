EVELUTION

FRAN HAYES & ZIHE ZHENG

According to technologist Blaise Agüera y Arcas, life is computational from its very inception; it emerges from systems that can self-replicate and transmit the information required to sustain existence. Under this lens, we are made of computers. We are computers. We are computers made of computers.
Yet, life is also inherently social, messy, and deeply entangled with all kinds of living and non-living kin. We are not isolated processing units; we are assemblages. How do we reconcile these two states? How can we be self-replicating computational systems that are simultaneously embedded within, and reliant upon, an ecology of infinitely different flavours of computation?
To bridge this divide, we turn to symbiogenesis, the coming-together of distinct parts to form a more complex whole[^1]. We turn the heat on, and let the soup cook.
As an entry point, a speculative symbiogenetic entity was conceived; an assemblage composed of the human mitochondrial genome (colloquially known as Mitochondrial Eve[^2]), the traditionally feminine craft of crochet, JavaScript code, and slime mold. In doing so, it is possible to actively question the strict, rigid, closed spaces that encircle computation, issues explained in the publication DATA LOAM: “the rise of autonomous systems whose algorithms of machine learning / artificial intelligence are deeply entangled with dubious and disintegrating forces—forces that by no means wish to make possible the prosperity and happiness of all.”[^3]
Can viewing the coming-together of different bodies[^4] through a radically feminine, craft-based lens offer a slower, kinder alternative to the relentless, extractive pace of current technological trends?
The text that follows is a conversation conducted across time, space, and species.
