EVELUTION

FRAN HAYES & ZIHE ZHENG

According to technologist Blaise Agüera y Arcas, life is computational from its very inception; it emerges from systems that can self-replicate and transmit the information required to sustain existence. Under this lens, we are made of computers. We are computers. We are computers made of computers.
Yet, life is also inherently social, messy, and deeply entangled with all kinds of living and non-living kin. We are not isolated processing units; we are assemblages. How do we reconcile these two states? How can we be self-replicating computational systems that are simultaneously embedded within, and reliant upon, an ecology of infinitely different flavours of computation?
To bridge this divide, we turn to symbiogenesis, the coming-together of distinct parts to form a more complex whole[^1]. We turn the heat on, and let the soup cook.
As an entry point, a speculative symbiogenetic entity was conceived; an assemblage composed of the human mitochondrial genome (colloquially known as Mitochondrial Eve[^2]), the traditionally feminine craft of crochet, JavaScript code, and slime mold. In doing so, it is possible to actively question the strict, rigid, closed spaces that encircle computation, issues explained in the publication DATA LOAM: “the rise of autonomous systems whose algorithms of machine learning / artificial intelligence are deeply entangled with dubious and disintegrating forces—forces that by no means wish to make possible the prosperity and happiness of all.”[^3]
Can viewing the coming-together of different bodies[^4] through a radically feminine, craft-based lens offer a slower, kinder alternative to the relentless, extractive pace of current technological trends?
The text that follows is a conversation conducted across time, space, and species. It brings together three distinct perspectives: [MITO]: The symbiogenic organism herself; [FRAN & ZIHE]: The human developers and companion-creators of the organism; [FUTURE SWARM]: A speculative, future, hive-mind evolution of the crochet cyborg, perhaps a symbiont of its own making.
Through this polyphonic dialogue, we intend to interrogate the motives and methods behind the creation of the cyborg, to de-center its human developers, and to ask whether decentering more previously dominant parties within our technological frameworks can shift us toward a future of deeper computational empathy and ecological respect.

In-Between States

Zhuzhu

Assemblage, Repetition, and the Labour of Technical Objects in Artistic Practice

Technical objects are often considered finished artifacts that serve a pre-determined purpose. Within an engineering logic, a system is expected to operate according to governed protocols in order to minimize errors. Control is essential. The system is designed to converge, to close the gap between its current state and its goal. Error is minimized. Noise is filtered. The technical object, under this logic, exists in service of a predetermined endpoint, and its success is measured by how efficiently it arrives there. Wiener called this cybernetics: the scientific study of control and communication in the animal and the machine.¹

If the endpoint is accepted as the goal of the work, then the entire process becomes instrumental. Every action is justified only by whether it gets you closer to the finished thing. The parts, the motors, the screws, the wires all become means, and the moment the object is complete, the process is discarded. This kills all the fun.

When I operate with a technical object, the boundary of clear authority over the system becomes intricate. I enter a space where control is shared, distributed, and often deliberately undermined. Engineering logic maximises control to converge toward a goal. Chance based art abandons control to generate complexity through randomness. Both produce a separation between the practitioner and the work: one makes the artist an executor of a plan, the other makes them a spectator to their own practice. I am interested in neither. I want to stay with the work in its various stages, sharing control with the system and sustaining that shared condition through repetition. The in between state proposed here occupies a third territory, a sustained condition where identity emerges through continuous differentiation rather than arrival at a predetermined form.

The exploded view performs something that I think Heidegger was getting at when he argued that equipment in use tends to withdraw from attention, and that only when it breaks down does its structure become visible.² To lay out the parts of a guitar across a surface is to make visible the relations that hold the object together. The joints. The screws. The connections that ordinarily withdraw into the unified form. Each part, once separated, reveals itself as a site of labour. Every screw has been tightened by someone. Every joint has been aligned by hand. The object is held together not by design alone but by accumulated acts of care.

The technical object does not exist only when assembled and functional. What Simondon calls phase is useful here: "it is the actual system of all phases taken together that is the complete reality, not each phase in itself."³ The object exists equally when its parts are distributed, when its connections are loosened, when its form is undone. The distributed parts carry the memory of having been one, and the potential of becoming one again, differently. The assembled guitar is a momentary stabilization, already anticipating its own disassembly. I have come to think of this as the object's real condition, always somewhere between together and apart, always in the middle of being maintained.

The question that keeps returning to me is one that extends what Ingold asks in his critique of the hylomorphic model of creation.⁴ He asks: what does it mean to create things? I want to push this further: what does it mean to maintain things? Because the shift from creation to maintenance changes everything. It means the work is never finished. It means the encounter with material is ongoing. It means the labour of repetition, adjustment, and care is the practice itself rather than a stage on the way to a completed object.

Ukeles understood this when she wrote her Manifesto for Maintenance Art in 1969.⁵ She drew a line between development and maintenance. Development labour produces a finished object and terminates. Maintenance labour is cyclical, endless, structurally incapable of completion. "Maintenance is a drag; it takes all the fucking time." I keep coming back to this sentence because it says something precise about the ontological condition of maintenance: it occupies all available time because it has no endpoint at which it could stop. To maintain a technical object is to enter a relationship with it that cannot be concluded. Every adjustment invites the next adjustment. Every tightened screw will loosen again.

The parts are not passive in this. They carry forward what has happened to them. Their accumulated wear, their loosened connections, their particular resistances shape what the next assembly can become. What Bolt calls material thinking, the idea that the practitioner does not think first and then make but thinks through the making itself, converges here with what Stiegler calls prosthetic memory, the way technical objects store experience outside the body.⁶ ⁷ The guitar parts are not inert matter waiting for my plan. They think back. They remember what my hands have done to them, and they push back in ways that change what I can do next. I am not the sole author of the configuration. The parts co-author it through their memory.

The artist, then, is a maintainer. Someone who stays with the system, shares control with it, and sustains the conditions under which difference can continue to emerge. Not an author who imposes form. Not a spectator who sets up conditions and steps back. A maintainer.

I should be honest about the limits of this position. If "refusing to finish" becomes a fixed formula, then the refusal of closure becomes its own form of closure. The methodology calcifies into style. The repetition stops producing difference and starts producing habit. I do not have a theoretical solution to this. The only safeguard is practical: genuine attention to whether the repetition is still producing difference or has become mechanical. Care cannot be guaranteed by a model. It can only be committed to and noticed when it fails.

What sustains all of this is care. To maintain is to repeat. To repeat is to differentiate. To differentiate is to remain in between. And to remain in between, deliberately, with attention and persistence, is to care.

The Vampire Machine: AI in the creation of art

Jemila Abdulai

Why AI art does not sit well with me

I vaguely remember the first time I came across an artwork made with AI. It was a short film with impressive graphics. It had vibrant and captivating moving images that morphed from one scene to the next. I was in awe. I began to feel the shared sense of excitement in the air at the time, excited for the possibilities for making my own short films.
As someone who at the time never thought I could ever make these impressive and technically complex artworks myself, AI seemed like the perfect avenue for me to get closer to a world I had always wanted to be a part of.

Now about two years later, I have moved quite far onto the opposite side of the spectrum about the use of AI models in art. In fact, I often find myself feeling a negative reaction I can't quite articulate properly yet, when I come across these same kinds of artworks browsing online or on social media today.

The term ‘AI’ (Artificial Intelligence) has been used to describe ‘the capability of computational systems to perform tasks typically associated with human intelligence, such as learning, reasoning, problem-solving, perception, and decision-making.’ However in recent years, due to the rise of Generative AI models such as ChatGPT and Midjourney, AI is known for a different purpose- making art.

Upon its initial introduction to the masses, Generative AI models seemed to be the future. The newest breakthrough in human technology, the ‘inevitable’ turn in human history that was supposed to fix all our problems.
Suddenly it was everywhere, and as someone who was always more of a techno-optimist, I thought this was a great turn of events.

All too soon however, AI was everywhere – and not in a good way.
Seemingly, every new tech startup launched was now an AI startup; and established companies that typically had nothing to do with technology were suddenly making ‘AI-driven strategy’ pivots; almost like they were trying to wedge AI assistants into any part of their business model they could.

On social media, there were many more ads pushing all the ways this shiny new technology made life so much better; with some even advertising a frankly dystopian product called ‘Friend’. This is a pendant embedded with an AI ‘companion’ you could wear around your neck and served as a partner to converse with day-to-day. Needless to say, it was not very well received by a lot of people, judging from the vandalism and graffiti it received on its ads around the city of New York.

Even in my personal life, the name ‘ChatGPT’ was everywhere, with people starting to refer to the LLM using anthropomorphised terms like ‘He/She’. Even more alarmingly, it was becoming more normalised to ask it for advice about things like where to visit on holiday, what to eat for dinner or even what to do about the issues you were having with a friend. With this in mind, I started to realise how embedded it was becoming in our day to day lives; and often not by choice.

Within the world of art, the ‘AI aesthetic’ emerged and along with it came the realisation for many artists that their works were being stolen and used to train these models to produce content without their consent.

In light of this, the wave of anti-AI sentiment became even more visible within the creative spaces I navigated. It was more common to label someone as ‘not a real artist’ if they had used AI in creating an artwork. Even the mention of AI in a creative context was regularly met with hostility and disdain.

The topic has become something very polarising today, with still no clear consensus about the legality of using models that have been trained with stolen or copyrighted artworks to create art. Without a doubt however, it’s safe to say the impact of AI on artists has been quite profound.

The loss of intellectual property is another outcome of the AI boom that must be discussed. Due to the aforementioned lack of legal clarity of using AI to produce art works, notions of authorship are often unclear and debatable. The referenced paper ‘The GPT Judge’ further explores this topic in further detail.

Consequently, the phenomenon of cognitive offloading is another aspect of the AI boom that is increasingly becoming more and more evident.

The outcome is clear - AI is making us lazier; according to a paper published by Fayaz et al. For some artists, using AI sometimes means offloading the essential tasks of art making such as ideation and execution to these LLMs in the name of efficiency and optimisation.

It is my personal belief that the most important parts of being an artist are the ability to be able to develop ideas in a way that feels subversive and meaningful; by being able to question and break norms and potentially collaborate with others to do so. Art has largely been used as a vehicle for change in our society.

However it seems increasingly, some relatively renowned artists do not resonate with that, which to me was unexpected.

Upon listening to a talk by the artist called ‘niceaunties’ who almost predominantly uses AI tools to make their work, something seemed amiss to me. Hearing them talk about how much nicer it is to work with AI because it ‘doesn't get angry when you ask for changes’ or how ‘the line of production has shrunk and therefore become more efficient’ is something that feels out of place for a practice that seeks to challenge, explore and question things.

The artist here in question describes their own practice as intended to ‘rewrite the stories of women’ making them ‘unapologetically free’. However, it seemed ironic to me to use a generative AI tool like KlingAI or Seedance to ‘rewrite stories’ when a major problem with AI is that it is regressive in nature.

AI models are often trained to operate on probability - meaning they operate by comparing what ‘is’ to what has already been. Google itself mentions using the Bayesian model as part of training its models, leading us to believe that common AI models are trained to always bring the output back to what exists and what has been done before. The outcome of this current model is that a lot of art ends up looking the same, because these models are drawing from the same probability distributions and end up simply producing the ‘average sum’ of a piece of artwork, based on all of the ones that exist in its dataset.

In my opinion, this makes it quite static in nature. It easily becomes a restricting force for an artist wanting to try something new, because it is so heavily based on reading information from previously gathered datasets.
AI is not easily able to create new arguments for norms to be rewritten and deviated from. When artists use it, they often cannot break out from the norm and question things, which arguably is one of the core purposes for art creation.

A lot of modern artists increasingly rely on technology or even ‘non-human’ collaborators to produce ideas and make art which is not a problem in itself. As a computational artist myself, I believe combining art and technology is a wonderful way to allow artists to express themselves in ways they may have not been able to do before, and be more audacious with their work.
Jenna Sutela’s work ‘nimiia cétiï’ is a brilliant and imaginative example of this. Her work uses machine learning, neural networks and bacteria to create a narrative that is rich and unique.

On the other hand however, when the artist becomes increasingly detached from the ideation, execution and implementation of an artwork, one begins to wonder whether the outcome is truly something we can consider to be ‘art’.

From my point of view, we must first start by asking ‘what is art and is AI art actually art?’ Who is art for? Why does it need to be optimised?’.

Additionally, we must question what purpose the creation of art serves; is it solely for generating income or for questioning the norm and imagining the world in a different way?

In my opinion, art is a way to express our thoughts and emotions, to communicate, and to connect with one another on the basis of our shared human experience. It doesn't seem right as an artist to create your own echo chamber of thoughts and narratives, completely disconnected from the reality of day to day life; devoid of the quality of human experience, just because it’s ‘more efficient’.

I strongly believe in the idea that there is no efficiency in creation.
In an ideal world, there should be very little place for trying to be efficient in the creative process because efficiency and producing should not be the goal. The toil of ideation and creation for an artist is a worthwhile process that should not be fast-tracked. If we ‘try to do everything efficiently, we are turning off the power of serendipity’. As Dimacali mentions in their article on AI Art ‘just because we can, doesn't mean we should’.

AI is further perpetuating the capitalist mindset within art spaces that the purpose of creating art is for the market, or that production and scale are the end goal. The effect of this way of thinking on art and artists tries to rationalise it as a matter of fact.
Despite this, we need to keep reminding ourselves about the purpose that art is intended to serve, before allowing this mentality to further creep into spaces where they are actively causing damage.

Indeed, the impact of AI is a serious societal and needless to mention - environmental issue. Despite this, we must all tackle it with a shared vision to protect the rights of artists and the general developmental and intellectual well-being of our society.

All things considered, the first major steps we need to take as a society are to create regulations to protect artists and put safety measures in place that actually have the best interest of the wider community at the center. Leaving these decisions at the hands of the CEOs of big tech companies who seem to have less and less interest in being humane and socially conscious is a guaranteed way for things to spin even more out of control.

Before we accept using AI in the artistic creation process, AI models as they are need a massive overhaul. No more should they be these massive black holes of data, consuming any form of input to the extent of even cannibalising themselves. Perhaps it might be better to create smaller, more intentional models whose data sets have been more ethically produced and gathered. Models created for a specific purpose, either by the artists themselves or in collaboration with others in their network.
The purpose of technology should not be trying to ‘solve’ art and make it repeatable or scalable; it should be to provide artists with a way of expressing themselves that they didn't have before.

Although AI currently seems to be advancing at a speed that feels overwhelming to imagine attempting to control and regulate, we all deserve to live, work and create in spaces that intrinsically support, rather than detract from our creativity and expression; and therefore our human experience itself.

Bypassing

Yiran Jiao

An Empty Judgment Produced by Technology

Imagine that one day, you are told that an algorithmic risk system has placed you on a watchlist. Your past records, your place of residence, your social relations: this set of quantifiable features is close enough to those of a certain high-risk group; and so, even though you may not have committed any illegal act, you still receive a determination about yourself: a score, a risk level, contact from the police. This system, Chicago's "Strategic Subject List," ran for several years before it was shut down.¹ So what I want to ask is: why can an operational result about an individual be produced before that individual's concrete action has even taken place, and change the way he is treated?

¹ The "Strategic Subject List" is taken here as an example because it is a closed case that has already run its full course and can be looked back on; but this logic did not end when it was shut down. Similar predictive tracking, targeting, and risk-determination systems are still widely deployed today across different contexts. What this essay is concerned with is their shared structure, not the specific circumstances of any single deployment.

  1. Judgment

To see clearly what this system does to judgment, we must first describe the old structure of judgment. Following Mireille Hildebrandt's account of the "informational" character of law, modern legal judgment is organized around actions that have already taken place, not around some persistent state of the subject. Concrete action is the object of judgment: a person can anticipate in advance that signing a contract and then failing to perform it may bring liability, precisely because the connection between "action and consequence" lets the meaning of a behaviour be grasped before it unfolds and confirmed after it unfolds. Reasons are the mediation through which an action can be interpreted; a transfer of money may be fraud, may be support of a dependent, may be repayment of a debt, and the same external behaviour points to different structures of responsibility under different reasons. Context is the background through which action and reasons can be read. These three together support the whole structure.

At the same time, Hildebrandt adds a feature that is crucial to what follows: judgment can be modified. It is not a result that forms once and is thereby closed, but a process continually reopened by new actions, facts, and explanations. The subject's later behaviour flows back, and can loosen the initial handling. Even an everyday prejudgment based on accent or clothing is no more neutral than an algorithmic score; but it takes place within a concrete scene, and a person can resist it with action. This capacity to be reopened is precisely what keeps judgment open to the correction of itself.

  1. "Judgment"

Now return to the "Strategic Subject List." Its operational logic is to infer future risk from past data. A person's records, location, risk of victimisation, and social relations are entered into the model, in order to calculate the possibility that he will become a shooting victim or perpetrator in the future. The person placed on the list may never have carried out any violent act; he may not even have done anything in the present scene that needs to be examined. He enters the system only because his data position is close enough to that of a certain high-risk group. What Antoinette Rouvroy and Thomas Berns call the "profile" comes into play here: what the system makes contact with is a statistical position recomposed from digital traces and everyday trajectories. These materials strictly belong to the past, and often do not belong to the subject's own actions: a high score may originate from the neighbourhood he lived in a few years ago, or from several records of co-appearing with a marked person. It is thin in attribution. What it records is a network position. What the subject did as an agent barely enters the calculation. And so the three conditions are absent one by one. Concrete action disappears: the basis for handling this person is a calculated closeness to future risk, and has nothing to do with what he did. Reasons disappear: what the model gives is correlation, a relation of co-occurrence between data points, and it cannot be refuted or corrected the way an interpretation of reasons can. Context disappears, or rather is displaced: the background is past data compressed into features, and it often belongs to a group: the crime rate of a neighbourhood, the statistical profile of a category of population, while the consequences fall on the individual.

And that capacity to "be reopened" is also absorbed in reverse. If a person marked as high-risk later carries out no violence, this does not overturn the handling of him. It can be read as the risk having never existed, or as the risk having already been preemptively intervened in, and either way, the score is preserved. The subject's later behaviour, his appeals, and even the fact that "the predicted behaviour never happened" are all absorbed as inputs into the model's next round of self-optimisation. What once could reopen judgment is converted into material that makes the system more precise and therefore more impossible to hold accountable. The channel of correction becomes a device that reinforces the initial handling.

This is the structure I want to call "empty judgment." Judgment is still operating, complete and efficient, with classification, contact, intervention, and consequences all falling on the subject. What has been hollowed out are the conditions on which judgment once stood: the action that was its object, the reasons that were its mediation, the context that let it be read, and the openness that kept it modifiable. The operation continues; the ground beneath its feet is no longer there. The occurrence of judgment no longer passes through the subject, no longer needs the subject's building and repair; instead, an external eye examines and measures the distance between the subject and each of the standards.

What technological mediation removes is not arbitrary. The conditions that are stripped away: concrete action, present context, the backflow of correction—are precisely those that depend on a span of time that has not yet arrived, that depend on an event still waiting to unfold. They cannot be computed in advance, because they have not yet happened. What the system bypasses is exactly these, while letting the part that can be computed in advance bear, on its own, the operational weight of the whole event. The SSL is only the clearest example of this; the same process applies to any system that has a high degree of operational access to the subject: credit scoring, recidivism prediction, risk profiling, all alike. What is finally left is a judgment that still falls fully on the person, while the conditions that once made it a judgment rather than a label imposed from outside have been bypassed.

And these bypassed conditions are not merely the technical parts of judgment. Concrete action, statable reasons, correction that can flow back. They are precisely the qualification by which a person is treated as a subject who can argue back, can respond, can be understood anew. When judgment can take effect as usual while bypassing them, what is suspended is not only certain conditions of judgment, but the subject's position to speak within this judgment.

Together, the two projects position recognition and opacity not as simple opposites, but as two conditions within the same encounter between machine vision and non-human life. Recognition can interrupt indifference and bring an overlooked life into attention. Yet complete legibility may also reduce that life to the categories through which a system is able to recognise it.

More powerful hardware, faster cameras, larger datasets or different detection models may eventually reduce, or even remove, many of the failures observed in the project. The significance of these failures therefore does not depend on their permanence. During this brief interval, the pigeon remains within the camera’s field of view, yet the model can no longer hold its moving body within a stable computational form. As the wing folds, accelerates and changes its outline, recognition is momentarily suspended. The bird does not leave the technological system. Instead, its movement opens a fold within recognition itself: a moment in which it remains present, but not fully resolved.

The_Unarchivable

Yumi

To most passers-by, pigeons appear almost identical. They belong to the familiar background of the city: birds gathering on pavements, rooftops and window ledges, visible almost everywhere but rarely noticed as individuals. The Unarchivable began by interrupting this habitual form of attention. It centred on Huihui and Diandian, two homing pigeons raised by the artist. Although they lived under human care and had individual names and histories, their appearance closely resembled that of the anonymous pigeons encountered across the city. Their position therefore remained uncertain: they were intimate and familiar lives, yet they also belonged visually to a population often treated as an interchangeable urban background.

The project asked whether technologies normally associated with classification, surveillance and identification could be redirected towards overlooked non-human life. Instead of detecting only the general category “pigeon,” could a machine-vision system learn to recognise Huihui and Diandian as separate individuals? Could this process create a space in which non-human life became visible, recordable and open to broader discussion?

Photographs and videos of the two pigeons were selected, cropped, organised and labelled to form a bespoke dataset. A YOLO object-detection model was then trained to distinguish Huihui, Diandian and other pigeons. YOLO frames object detection as a regression problem in which a neural network predicts bounding boxes and associated class probabilities from an image (Redmon et al. 2016, 779–80). It does not encounter a bird as a living subject. Instead, the image is processed as numerical input and translated into coordinates, class labels and confidence values.

Recognition is therefore produced through abstraction. Visual differences are transformed into measurable features and compared with the categories established during training. As Trevor Paglen argues, machine-readable images increasingly operate through classifications, training sets and mathematical abstractions rather than through forms of vision addressed primarily to human spectators (Paglen 2016). What appears through the interface as the recognition of a stable object is consequently the visible result of a series of computational operations.

This process reveals the double function of classification. Classification can reduce a complex life to a limited category that a technical system is able to process. Yet in The Unarchivable, it also created an opportunity. The model was not asked simply to detect “a pigeon.” Huihui and Diandian entered the dataset as separate classes, requiring their differences to become computationally meaningful. By separating them from the general category of pigeon, the project attempted to interrupt the anonymity imposed on urban non-human life.

The trained model operated as a speculative form of digital identification. A technology commonly used to locate and categorise objects was redirected towards two lives usually excluded from individual records. In this sense, machine vision became not only a tool of detection but also a method of sustained attention.

Yet this attention remained conditional. Huihui and Diandian could be recognised only through the photographs selected for the dataset, the labels assigned to them and the visual patterns learned from those examples. The model did not discover their identities independently. It produced them computationally through the conditions constructed by the dataset. When their bodies no longer corresponded closely enough to these learned patterns—because of rapid movement, overlap, occlusion or changes in silhouette—the stability of recognition began to break down.

Learning to Fold developed from this contradiction rather than from a rejection of the earlier project. The two works form reciprocal moments within the same investigation. The Unarchivable asks what machine recognition can make visible; Learning to Fold examines the conditions under which that visibility becomes unstable. The technology that grants an individual position to the bird also reveals its limits when the body can no longer be maintained as a coherent and readable object.

During the first project, detection was repeatedly disrupted when the pigeons moved rapidly, overlapped with one another or entered visually complex environments. Bounding boxes flickered, shifted, multiplied or disappeared. Learning to Fold isolates one recurring source of this instability: the folding and unfolding of a pigeon’s wing. The same YOLO object-detection framework was used to examine how rapid transformation, self-occlusion and changing silhouettes affected computational recognition.

The project distinguishes embodied seeing from computational abstraction and classification. Embodied seeing involves an encounter situated within a perceptual and meaningful world. Computational abstraction, by contrast, translates visual variation into quantifiable features, while classification assigns those features to predefined categories. Machine recognition combines the latter operations, even though its interface often presents the outcome as though the machine had directly perceived a stable object (Paglen 2016).

The project develops the term algorithmic disorientation to describe moments when this calculation cannot be maintained consistently across successive frames.¹ “Disorientation” does not imply that the algorithm is conscious, confused or capable of subjective experience. Rather, it names an operational condition in which the system loses the spatial and temporal consistency necessary to produce the appearance of one coherent object moving through time. The calculations continue, but bounding boxes compete for position, confidence values fall, and the detected object may fragment into several uncertain presences.

To investigate this condition, Learning to Fold moves away from the complete pigeon and focuses on a custom-built skeletal wing structure. The structure abstracts the wing into hinges, extensions and folding relations, allowing selected movements to be repeated under more controlled conditions. It does not claim to reproduce pigeon anatomy. Instead, it functions as an experimental device for testing folding, vibration, acceleration, concealment and changes in outline.

These detection failures cannot be attributed to one cause. Within the experiments, folding produced overlap and concealed previously visible features. Speed introduced motion blur and greater visual differences between consecutive frames. Lighting, camera frame rate, object scale, processing latency and the limits of the training data also affected the model’s outputs. The project therefore does not claim that pigeon wings are intrinsically or permanently beyond computation. Improved cameras, larger datasets, faster hardware or different model architectures might reduce many of these errors. What matters is the temporary interval in which the system used in the project cannot stabilise the moving body as a continuous computational identity.

Henri Bergson’s concept of duration helps to frame this interval. Bergson distinguishes lived duration from spatialised models of time that divide movement into separate and measurable units. In duration, successive moments do not exist as isolated points but flow into and transform one another (Bergson 2001, 100–106). Object detection, by contrast, samples movement through separate frames and reconstructs continuity from a sequence of individual predictions. Flickering and multiplying boxes expose moments when this reconstruction no longer appears seamless. The wing continues moving, but the model’s account of it becomes discontinuous.

Building on these ideas, the project develops the term temporal opacity.² Édouard Glissant’s right to opacity challenges the assumption that difference must become fully transparent or comprehensible in order to enter into relation. Opacity does not simply mean invisibility, isolation or withdrawal. It protects that which cannot be entirely reduced to the terms through which another person or system seeks to understand it (Glissant 1997, 189–94).

Zach Blas extends this concern into computational culture through the concept of informatic opacity. Blas examines opacity in relation to systems that convert identity, difference and behaviour into readable and classifiable information (Blas 2018, 197–99). His account is particularly relevant to technologies whose operation depends on rendering bodies computationally legible.

Temporal opacity is not a term borrowed directly from either Glissant or Blas. Rather, it is developed through their work to describe unstable legibility across successive moments of object detection. Learning to Fold brings opacity into the temporal operation of machine vision. The wing is detected in one frame, lost in another and multiplied in the next. Its presence is not absent, but unevenly legible.

Temporal opacity therefore does not prove that life will always exceed technology, nor does it turn the pigeon into a conscious political resistor. It describes a provisional condition produced through the relationship between a moving body, a camera, a dataset and an object-detection model. While this instability lasts, the bird remains visibly present without being fully maintained as one stable computational object.

Together, the two projects position recognition and opacity not as simple opposites, but as two conditions within the same encounter between machine vision and non-human life. Recognition can interrupt indifference and bring an overlooked life into attention. Yet complete legibility may also reduce that life to the categories through which a system is able to recognise it.

More powerful hardware, faster cameras, larger datasets or different detection models may eventually reduce, or even remove, many of the failures observed in the project. The significance of these failures therefore does not depend on their permanence. During this brief interval, the pigeon remains within the camera’s field of view, yet the model can no longer hold its moving body within a stable computational form. As the wing folds, accelerates and changes its outline, recognition is momentarily suspended. The bird does not leave the technological system. Instead, its movement opens a fold within recognition itself: a moment in which it remains present, but not fully resolved.

Re-Sensing Locality

Qiang Hei

DRIFTING as a Remote Signal

Abstract

This article develops from my practice-based research project DRIFTING. The project responds to the transformation of my family’s home village, Quanergo Village in Shanxi, China. This village was once shaped by abundant water resources, close kinship relations, and everyday rural life. However, under the combined effects of coal mining, groundwater depletion, population outflow, and wind power infrastructure, Quanergo has gradually been reorganised as a spatial node within an energy production network. Rather than understanding this transformation simply as the “disappearance of place”, this article asks: when a specific place is incorporated into the larger systems of modernisation and technological infrastructure, can locality still be re-sensed?

I argue that locality should not be understood as a fixed geographical entity, but as a dynamic perceptual network generated through multiple relations. It is constituted through the interactions between natural environment, local memory, social relations, industrial infrastructure, and technological media systems.

In DRIFTING, a wind-direction sensor installed in Quanergo transmits real-time local wind-direction data across geographical distance to a Digital Earth system, where it guides a digital avatar to walk continuously across a virtual terrain on a global scale. The work does not attempt to reconstruct a complete, landscape-like image of the hometown in virtual space. Instead, it allows place to enter present perceptual experience as a weak, remote, yet continuously active digital signal.

Through this project, the article explores how small-scale and situated forms of computational art might offer a way to re-sense locality under the conditions of vast contemporary technological infrastructures. In this sense, computational art does not necessarily need to pursue larger and more controlling technological systems. It can also develop a poetic, situated, and micro-scale technological structure, through which marginalised local experiences obscured by modernisation processes and infrastructural landscapes may become perceptible again.

  1. Locality Under Infrastructure

Human geography has often understood locality as a distinctive experience formed within a particular region through its natural environment, historical memory, and social practices. However, in Quan’ergou, the large number of wind turbines and the decreasing number of permanent residents suggest that the transformation of place is not only a change in landscape, but also a change in the mode of production.
The “wind” of Quan’ergou no longer belongs only to local climatic experience, bodily sensation, or everyday life. Instead, it is woven into systems of energy transition, electricity transmission, and infrastructural construction. The village has not disappeared in a physical sense, but it is increasingly identified as a wind-energy resource, a construction space, a coordinate location, or an object of development.

As Benjamin Bratton discusses through the concept of “planetary-scale computation,” within digitized and infrastructural spatial structures, geographical space is not only the site where concrete life takes place; it is also incorporated into clouds, systems, interfaces, and data relations, becoming a node that can be identified, compared, and calculated. 1.The problem is not that place becomes completely invisible, but that it can often only be seen in certain specific ways: as resource, data, energy, productivity, or as a space waiting to be planned and renewed.

What can be recognized by the system is often land, wind energy, spatial use, and data relations; what is difficult to incorporate into the system includes kinship networks, oral memories, everyday practices, and the complex relations formed between people and specific spaces across particular historical periods. The crisis of locality lies in the fact that its physical space is continually developed, updated, and reorganized, while the historically generated knowledge, experience, and relations within it may gradually be forgotten through a series of ruptures.

Therefore, the obscuring of place by infrastructure does not simply mean erasing place, but changing the way place is understood and experienced.

  1. From Extractive Wind to Situated Wind

In response to this infrastructural logic, computational art does not necessarily need to respond with a larger technological scale. On the contrary, it can begin from small scale, locality, and incompleteness in order to re-establish the relationship between technology and place. Donna Haraway’s discussion of “situated knowledges” offers an important reminder here: knowledge does not come from a disembodied, all-seeing perspective, but is always generated within specific bodies, locations, relations, and technological conditions.2

A technological device that appears to be globally applicable is no longer merely an abstract device once it enters a specific place. The wind turbines on the mountains of Quan’ergou and the wind turbines offshore near London cannot simply be understood as the same object. Although they share similar industrial forms, they are embedded in entirely different geographical environments, historical processes, and social relations. For Quan’ergou, the wind turbines have already become part of the mountainous landscape, as well as visible traces of the complex relations between energy development, population outflow, spatial reorganization, and the rupture of local memory.

The wind turbines and the wind-direction sensor in DRIFTING form a key contrast. Both deal with “wind,” but the relationships they establish with place are not the same. The wind turbine transforms wind into electricity, output value, and transmissible energy, and is a form of extractive infrastructure. The wind-direction sensor, however, does not extract resources, nor does it attempt to compete with large-scale infrastructure at the level of energy. Instead, within the spatial gaps produced by infrastructural reorganization, it translates local wind into an action signal in a distant digital space, creating a lighter, more local, and more intimate entry point into local experience.

Therefore, small-scale situated technological practice is not an objective reproduction of place, but a method of approaching place again. The importance of technological connection lies precisely in the fact that it no longer remains at the level of an abstract discussion about “technology” or “place,” but unfolds within a specific location, coming into contact with environment, history, and social relations.

  1. Place as a Remote Signal: From Sensing to Re-sensing

In the mechanism of DRIFTING, a micro wind-direction sensor is deployed in Quan’ergou Village, transmitting data through a cloud server into a global networked system. The wind of Quan’ergou is no longer perceived only as a local natural phenomenon, but becomes a directional force that drives the drifting of a digital avatar. In this sense, the sensor does not simply record environmental data; rather, it participates in forming a new relation between a distant environment, a digital interface, and the audience’s present perception.3 Through this process, place is no longer viewed as a fixed image, but is re-sensed as a weak, remote, yet continuously active signal.

At the same time, the digital Earth system is transformed from a platform usually serving navigation and planning into an unstable interface disturbed by a local environmental signal. 4 The digital avatar may walk over mountains and cross oceans. It has no clear destination, nor does it follow the shortest path or an efficiency-oriented logic of navigation; instead, it continuously drifts through changing map landscapes. It is precisely within this local, limited, and fragile mechanism that a new perceptual relation is formed between Quan’ergou and the outside world.

The audience cannot directly arrive at Quan’ergou, nor can they fully view the village. Yet through a continuously changing signal, they are able to sense the influence of a distant environment on the present space. Locality is no longer buried as something belonging to the past, but is reactivated as a present continuous force: a force that remains at a distance while continuing to exert an effect.

From this, the artistic logic of this text can be summarized as follows: under the condition that large-scale technological systems already exist, it is possible to create a more specific, more local, and more intimate entry point into local experience, transforming the technological relations within place into small-scale and perceptible artistic interfaces. The task of computational art is not only to use technology to represent place, but to reconfigure the relationship between technology and place, and to produce new ways of sensing locality.

Conclusion: Locality as a Continuously Generated Dynamic Perceptual Network

For this text, Quan’ergou is not a static ruin waiting to be preserved, and DRIFTING is not a simple remembrance of a lost hometown. Together, they point to another, more realistic task: under the condition that place has already been deeply reorganized by global infrastructure, data logic, and processes of modernization, how can place still continue to operate as a force of experience? Locality, therefore, is not an archived past, but a continuously generated dynamic perceptual network; it is repeatedly activated between wind, data, memory, distance, and technological interfaces, and enters the present in an incomplete yet continuous form.

The Flâneur, Reflective Architecture, and the Circulation of Urban Images

Glass in the city sometimes makes people stop.

While walking through the city, glass curtain walls often bring the sky, streets, vehicles, pedestrians and surrounding buildings onto the same surface. Things that belong to different distances and spatial layers are compressed by reflection into a temporary flat image.

These reflections are usually unstable. They change with light, weather, viewing angle and the position of the passer-by. When the viewer moves forward or steps back, the image on the building surface also changes. In this sense, the reflective facade is not only an exterior material of architecture. It also takes part in organising how people look at the city.

This article follows what happens after such a reflected image appears. A cloud in the sky is first turned by the glass facade into an optical image. It may then be photographed by a smartphone, become a digital file, and finally enter cloud storage and networked platforms. In this process, computational media do not simply preserve the architectural reflection. They extend, modify and redistribute it.

The reflection, therefore, does not end on the glass surface. It moves from the physical city into computational systems, continuing to circulate across devices, files and platforms.

This kind of pause changes the act of walking. Moving through the city is no longer only a movement from one place to another. The viewer is interrupted by the image on the building surface and begins to adjust their position according to light, angle and reflection. A clear route is briefly disturbed, and another kind of route begins to appear: not one drawn by urban planning, but one organised by the facade, the viewer’s position and the temporary image that forms between them.

Walter Benjamin’s The Arcades Project offers a way to think about this kind of urban attention. Benjamin writes about the arcades of nineteenth-century Paris, where glass, iron, shop windows, commodities, crowds and artificial light formed a modern space between the street and the interior. The arcade was not simply a passage for movement, nor was it an ordinary room. Bodies passed through it; gazes moved across it; commodities were displayed; desire was organised; and the city became readable through fragments.

The figure of the flâneur is closely connected to this space. The flâneur is often understood as an urban wanderer, but this figure is not simply someone who walks around the city. The flâneur reads the city through pauses, turns and accidental attention. He does not begin from a total plan of the city, but from shop windows, mirrors, signs, light, crowds and surfaces. Wandering is not a lack of direction. It is a way of allowing what is encountered on the way to redirect attention.

If this way of looking is brought into the contemporary city, glass remains important, but its role has changed. In the arcade, glass framed commodities and mediated the relation between street and interior. In contemporary reflective facades, glass often hides the inside of the building and sends the outside world back to the viewer. Instead of seeing what happens inside the building, one sees the sky, roads, cars and one’s own body spread across its surface.

The reflective facade can therefore be understood as another kind of passage. It may not allow the body to pass through, but it allows light, urban space and image to move between different states. It is not simply a wall, nor only a mirror. It is an intermediate surface where reality begins to take the form of an image.

In this sense, architecture comes before the camera.

A reflective facade does not neutrally copy the outside world. The image it forms depends on the orientation of the building, the colour of the glass, the curve of the surface, the weather, the light and the viewer’s position. Flat glass may divide the sky into a grid. A curved facade may distort the shape of nearby buildings. Dark glass may alter the brightness of clouds and streets. If the viewer moves only a little, the spatial relation inside the reflection also changes.

The building is not merely an object inside a photograph. It has already produced an image before photography takes place. It cuts the sky, compresses distance, and places distant buildings, nearby cars, moving clouds and the body of the passer-by onto the same surface. The photographer does not face an untouched reality, but an image already organised by architecture, light and the city.

This also changes the position of the photographer. The photographer is not always an author first. They may first be a viewer.

The discussion of “audience photography” in Mirroring（te magazine Nº3，2024）offers a useful way to understand this. Photographing does not always begin with a fully active author. Sometimes the photographer is first turned into a viewer by a work, a space or a visual relation, and only afterwards participates through the act of taking a photograph. Photography does not start from an empty position. It continues a relation of looking that has already begun.

Photography of reflective facades works in a similar way. The urban wanderer is first drawn by the building surface. They stop, adjust their position, and observe how the reflection changes with the movement of the body. Only then is the phone lifted. The smartphone is not the starting point of the whole image process. It is the second transformation of an image already produced by the building.

This is one difference between reflective architecture and an ordinary urban view. In front of a normal wall, the viewer usually remains outside the architectural surface. In front of reflective glass, the viewer has already entered the image. Body, cloud, cars, trees and distant buildings appear together on the facade. The viewer looks at the city, but also becomes part of the city image.

This may help explain why reflective architecture often invites photography. A cloud in the sky is open and without a frame. It does not easily form a stable composition with the human body. The curtain wall gives it scale, division and position. Architecture seems to have done part of the photographic work in advance, turning a continuous and changing reality into an image waiting to be captured.

Yet the phone does not simply keep this image as it is. When the reflection is photographed, light enters the sensor and is converted into electronic signals. The phone processes exposure, colour, contrast and dynamic range. The image may be brightened, sharpened, compressed, encoded and attached to information such as time, location and device data. What appears on the screen may look like a record of what has just been seen, but it is already another version.

Architecture works with light and space.The phone works with signals and data. From glass to phone, the image shifts from an optical image to a computational image. The computation does not happen on the glass itself. It happens when the reflection is captured by the sensor, processed by software, encoded as a file and given metadata. A reflection that once depended on weather, angle and bodily presence becomes a digital object that can be copied, searched, edited and transmitted.

After the wanderer’s body leaves the site, the image can still continue to move. This is the process of moving from cloud to cloud.

The first cloud is a meteorological event in the sky. It is formed by water vapour, temperature and air movement. It changes continuously and moves with the weather. It can only be seen from a particular place and at a particular moment; it cannot truly be possessed.

The second cloud is the cloud of computational systems. It is not weather, but a structure of storage and access made of servers, data centres, cables, accounts, synchronisation systems and interfaces. It is called a cloud, as if it were light and immaterial, but it depends on very material infrastructures.

Between these two clouds, the image passes through a chain of transformations: the cloud in the sky becomes an optical reflection on glass; the reflection is captured by a phone; the phone turns it into a computational image; the image is encoded as a file; the file enters the data cloud and continues to circulate across devices and platforms.

The first cloud reaches architecture as light. The second cloud receives it as data.

This is not a simple jump from “reality” to “the virtual”. The image does not suddenly leave the physical world and enter a space without material conditions. It passes through architecture, device, account, address, interface and user, being registered, converted, stored and redistributed step by step. Benjamin’s arcade helps us understand how bodies moved through the glass spaces of the modern city. In the contemporary computational city, images also move through a system made of city, cloud, interface, address and user. Bratton’s account of layered computation is useful here not as an external theoretical frame, but as a way to see that once an image on a reflective facade is photographed, it no longer belongs only to the building site. It enters a continuous environment made by both urban space and computational space.

The reflective facade is therefore not outside the computational system. It is one of the surfaces through which the image begins to enter it. The facade organises reality into an optical image ready to be captured, while the phone and the cloud give that image a form that can be stored, synchronised and called up again.

Once the image enters the data cloud, it does not stop. It may be synchronised across several devices, placed in a digital album, attached to a time and location, uploaded to a social platform, or later arranged by the system as a “memory”. It may also be cropped, compressed, edited, forwarded, screenshotted and shown on someone else’s screen.

At this point, the movement of the image is no longer decided by weather, sunlight or viewing angle, but by file systems, platform interfaces, search, recommendation and archiving. The original reflection may have lasted only a few seconds. The digital image can reappear after the weather has changed and after the photographer has left the site.

This may be understood as the image’s second wandering. The first wandering belongs to the body. The flâneur moves through the city, stops in front of the building and finds the reflection.

The second wandering belongs to the image. It moves from glass to phone, from phone to file, from file to cloud and platform, and finally to the interfaces of other viewers.

These two forms of wandering are not separate. The body’s pause allows the image to be captured, while the later circulation of the image records where the body stopped, when it looked, what device it used, and how the image was stored or shared. While the wanderer records the city, the computational system also records the wanderer.

This marks an important difference between today’s wanderer and Benjamin’s flâneur. The nineteenth-century flâneur read modern life through the surfaces of the city. The contemporary wanderer also turns these surfaces into data. They are at once viewer and image producer; they build a personal visual archive while leaving traces of time, location and behaviour within platform systems. Authorship therefore becomes difficult to locate.

The building first forms the image. The viewer chooses a position. The phone processes the light. Software produces the file. The cloud stores the image. The platform arranges how it appears. Algorithms affect when, at what size and to whom it is shown. The making and circulation of the image are distributed across several actors and technical processes.

Architecture is the first producer of the image. The photographer is both viewer and participant. The phone carries out a second production. The cloud and platform take part in later storage, editing and distribution. This distributed relation also changes what it means to have seen something.

When a reflected image has passed through architecture, camera, software, file, cloud and platform, seeing is no longer a direct relation between the human eye and reality. The viewer first sees a city reorganised by the building. The phone then processes it as a digital image. Metadata gives it time and place. The cloud extends its duration. The platform gives it a new context for being seen.

What people later trust may not only be their memory of the site, but also the file, coordinates and timestamp recorded by the device. A photograph seems to prove that the moment happened. Yet what it proves is not an unmediated reality, but an image path that has passed through several surfaces and systems.

This does not mean that visual authority has completely moved from humans to machines. The viewer still decides where to stop, what to photograph, how to frame it, and whether to keep or share the image. But from the moment the shutter is pressed, the image’s mode of existence is no longer controlled by the viewer alone. It is shaped by architecture, sensor, software, cloud and platform together.

Seeing is not simply invalidated, nor is it fully handed over to the device. It is redistributed. Visual trust is formed between the eye, the glass, the camera, the file, the metadata, the cloud and the interface.

This is why the reflective facade needs to be understood again. It is not merely a background for photography, nor only an architectural surface that resembles a screen. It is closer to a first turning point through which urban images enter computational systems. It allows sky, architecture, body and street to become an optical image, and then allows that image to move into devices, files and clouds.

From cloud to cloud is not a simple route of preservation. It is a media chain in which the image changes its state, its authorship and its conditions of being seen. The flâneur moves through the city. The image moves through architecture, device and computational system.

What continues to exist in the second cloud is not a simple copy of the first cloud. It is another version produced by nature, architecture, photography and computation together. In this version, a contemporary way of seeing the city becomes clearer: people still walk through the street, but images have learned to keep wandering through systems.

Moving from Technofeudal Enclosure to Techno-Socialist Infrastructures

Stathis

Part I: The Mutation of Capital

When digital platforms first captured the public imagination in the early 2010s, they presented themselves as the ultimate architecture of direct democracy, a borderless, horizontal tool empowering individuals to share their voices globally. Today, that utopian landscape has been entirely enclosed. The digital sphere is ruled by a consolidated cartel of tech behemoths: Google, Amazon, Meta, Apple, and Microsoft (GAMAM). These entities no longer operate as standard market competitors; rather, as Mark Zuckerberg once candidly observed of Facebook, they function "more like a government than a traditional company" (Foer 2017; Törnberg 2023).

What we have witnessed over the last decade is not the maturation of neoliberal capitalism, but its profound, systemic mutation into technofeudalism. Dominant tech giants have constructed digital ecosystems that resemble feudal societies, reigning over vast data "fiefdoms" while everyday users are effectively reduced to "digital serfs" trading personal data for access to basic social logistics (Saura García 2024). This structural shift has led scholars to define our contemporary reality through new political topologies, ranging from "Datafeudalism" (Saura García 2024) to Yanis Varoufakis’s (2023) definitive diagnosis of "Technofeudalism".

Part II: Cloud Rent and the Data Labourer

To understand the immense asymmetries of power in this new era, we must examine how these platforms extract profit. Technofeudal platforms operate by extracting wealth through a new economic mechanism that Varoufakis (2023, 127–130) terms "cloud rent". Much like medieval landlords who profited from ground rent without producing goods, digital landlords like Amazon or Apple take a mandatory cut from any business relying on their digital infrastructure to reach consumers. The open, competitive marketplace of classic capitalism has been replaced by a digital fiefdom where "vassal capitalists" must pay continuous tribute to the platform overlords simply to participate in the online economy (Varoufakis 2023).

The core strategy driving this rentier extraction is attention capture. Rather than selling discrete products, platforms deploy algorithm-driven feeds, personalised recommendations, and AI assistants to subtly modify user behaviour and keep individuals locked inside their "everything apps" (Foer 2017; Varoufakis 2023).

If we examine this through a classic Marxist economic lens, wherein the economic value of a commodity is determined by the total socially necessary labour time required to produce it, we unlock the fundamental secret of the platform economy. In the digital age, data is the supreme raw material. Therefore, the everyday hours users spend scrolling, posting, clicking, and navigating constitute a new, uncompensated form of socially necessary labour time. Under the engineered illusion of "free" entertainment, the user becomes both the product and the unpaid workforce.

Because this dynamic constitutes an unequal and exploitative exchange, theorists E. Glen Weyl and Eric Posner (2018), alongside Imanol Arrieta Ibarra et al. (2018), argue that platform capitalism has generated massive "missing markets". Because there is no established market mechanism to price or financially compensate individuals for the data they generate, tech monopolies reap 100% of the financial surplus. To correct this failure, Weyl, Posner, and Ibarra suggest that society must expand beyond traditional trade unionism and organise into a massive, global "data labour movement". If data is the primary, uncompensated resource used to train today's hyper-profitable AI algorithms, then the everyday internet user is a frontline labourer, and the data worker must be paid.

Part III: The Material Enclosure of Nature and Computation

Because this essay appears under the banner of Ecology and Society, we must dismantle the prevailing Silicon Valley myth that the "cloud" is an ethereal, weightless, and ecologically neutral phenomenon. Technofeudalism is an aggressively material, ecologically destructive project that burns physical resources to maintain digital enclosure.

We see this physical enclosure clearly in the hardware obsolescence inherent in the modern corporate subscription model. When a consumer purchases a physical asset like a Tesla vehicle, the physical car is only a fraction of the commodity. The vehicle functions as a localised node in a vast digital network, continuously feeding Big Data capital back to the central platform (Varoufakis 2023). If the driver ceases to pay their ongoing software tribute, the platform arbitrarily revokes autopilot capabilities, battery management updates, and performance monitoring. The physical machine is deliberately rendered obsolete via the cloud. When we multiply this software lock-in across smart urban grids, agricultural machinery, and personal computing, we see an endless-growth model that generates mountains of e-waste simply to guarantee the collection of cloud rent.

Furthermore, the logic of digital extraction has actively spilled over into the financialisation of the natural world. Consider the Ant Forest app, a digital platform introduced by Alibaba that gamifies users' carbon footprints, awarding green credits and scores that translate into real-world tree planting initiatives as users consume goods within Alibaba's e-commerce ecosystem. On the surface, this appears to be a progressive socio-ecological tool. However, as researcher Giulia Dal Maso (2022) warns, applications like Ant Forest serve primarily to expand the reach of financial capitalism into our private environmental behaviours. By quantifying ecological consciousness into a gamified consumption metric, the platform recalibrates accountability. It actively shifts the systemic responsibility for climate breakdown away from massive industrial conglomerates and the state, placing the moral burden entirely onto individual consumer discipline (Dal Maso 2022). The industrial systems driving ecological collapse are left to operate without restriction, while the platform extracts further behavioral data and moral capital from the user.

This non-consensual extraction of human energy reaches its absolute peak in planetary-scale computation. To see how this operates in the live AI debate, we need look no further than Niantic Inc. and its mobile gaming sensation, Pokémon Go (Schneider 2026). For the past decade, tens of millions of players walking through city streets voluntarily submitted photos and videos through their smartphone cameras, unknowingly acting as an unpaid global cartography workforce. This massive crowdsourcing effort generated a proprietary visual archive of 30 billion highly accurate, geotagged images (Schneider 2026). Niantic Spatial subsequently captured this uncompensated leisure labour to train a Large Geospatial Model (LGM), a photorealistic, 3D digital model of the physical world sold exclusively to enterprise robotics and autonomous corporate machines (Hayden 2026). The human being is gamified to map the physical globe for free, generating the exact AI capital required to power the autonomous systems that will displace human labour.

Part IV: Algorithmic Governmentality and the Cloud Polis

This relentless drive for volume hegemony exacts an equally devastating toll on our political ecology. As García (2024) argues, digital platforms increasingly function as privatised public spheres where a handful of tech monopolies govern the flow of global information. Drawing directly upon Jürgen Habermas’s classic 1962 critique, Saura García (2024, 10) diagnoses a dangerous "re-feudalisation of the public sphere," wherein powerful corporate entities monopolise discourse and restrict genuine public participation. Concurrently, Shoshana Zuboff (2015, 86) warns that by transforming the open public square into a profit-driven ecosystem, tech giants dictate the discursive limits of democracy, commodifying public debate into a controlled corporate asset.

We have seen the catastrophic fallout of this corporate knowledge advantage in recent electoral history. From the weaponisation of scraped user data by Cambridge Analytica in the 2016 US presidential election (Lewis and Hilder 2018) to the overt, algorithmic amplification of political agendas by tech moguls like Elon Musk on platform X during the 2024 elections, Big Tech has politicised its infrastructure.

This influence is not exercised through explicit persuasion or transparent editorial bias; rather, it operates through what Antoinette Rouvroy defines as algorithmic governmentality (Rouvroy and Stiegler 2016; Rouvroy 2020). By processing massive datasets, the platform manages the social world by pre-emptively shaping our informational environment, nudging human behaviour below the threshold of conscious thought. It achieves this through "volume hegemony", overwhelming the digital archive with a specific narrative until feed algorithms privilege it as objective truth. Because the interface moves at hyper-velocity, it forces users to react on raw impulse before they can achieve conscious reflexivity (Rouvroy 2020). This constant commercial diet of outrage keeps users on the apps longer to generate advertising rent, locking society into a paralysing state of "national despair" (Taplin 2026).

We are left standing on a profound political threshold. The tech giants have vastly outgrown the definitions of private enterprise. As Benjamin Bratton (2015) outlines in his structural model of the Cloud Polis, mega-platforms already function as sovereign proto-states. They have absorbed traditional state assignments such as global cartography, legal identity verification, and private market governance, binding users to Terms of Service that supersede national constitutions (Bratton 2015).

This brings us to the conceptual horizon of my essay. A democratic society cannot survive if its primary spaces of discourse, commerce, and logistics are owned by unaccountable tech overlords. If Big Tech already functions as an essential infrastructure and a privatised proto-state, we must stop trying to politely persuade or regulate it within a neoliberal framework. We must begin transitioning these platforms into genuine public goods.

Part V: The Techno-Socialist Counter-Offensive

To break this cycle of AI enclosure and cloud rent, we must realise that the architecture of our digital future is not predetermined. The profound crisis of platform capitalism is fundamentally a crisis of ownership and civic imagination. We are not trapped inside the Cloud Polis; the tools required to dismantle technofeudalism and construct a techno-socialist reality already exist. I argue that our counter-offensive relies on two massive structural pillars, platform cooperativism and the open-source commons, supported by the prefigurative, small-scale models of resistance already operating on the live web.

The first and most vital structural pillar is platform cooperativism. If the core problem of technofeudalism is the top-down extraction of surplus value by distant cloudalists, the direct structural solution is democratic, collective ownership. A platform cooperative operates with the exact same sleek, scalable technological capability as a Silicon Valley startup, but it is owned and governed democratically by the very workers and users who depend on it.

We see this structural alternative thriving today in networks like CoopCycle, a European federation of bike delivery cooperatives. Instead of subjecting riders to the opaque, wage-skimming algorithmic management of corporate giants, CoopCycle provides its members with open-source digital logistics infrastructure. Crucially, the fees for server maintenance are decided democratically by the workers themselves, and the software is protected by a unique "Coopyleft" licence, legally ensuring the technology can only be used to benefit self-managed collectives. It proves that digital logistics do not require an exploitative business model to scale.

The second foundational pillar is the broader open-source movement. To eliminate cloud rent, the underlying code of our contemporary society must be returned to the public commons. When software is released from restrictive, proprietary licences, communities retain the vital power to inspect visibility algorithms, maintain data privacy, and build decentralized services without paying tribute to a tech landlord. We already possess undeniable proof that non-extractive, peer-to-peer production can dominate globally. Look at Wikipedia, a planetary-scale repository of human knowledge that operates entirely through public donations, deliberately rejecting advertising rent and algorithmic manipulation. Look at Blender, an industry-standard 3D creation suite sustained entirely by mutual aid and open collaboration rather than corporate hierarchy.

While open-source protocols provide the foundation and cooperatives provide the economic engine, we must also look to small-scale prefigurative examples that show us how to live outside Big Tech right now. A profound example of this is the development of autonomous feminist servers. Created as a direct response to the non-consensual data extraction and hypocritical censorship of multinational tech monopolies, feminist servers are small-scale, autonomous digital infrastructures built to preserve community projects and collective memory. Operating on an "ethic of care," they utilize alternative legal frameworks like the Feminist Peer Production Licence, which strictly forbids commercial exploitation by capitalist enterprises. Though operating at a smaller scale, these servers serve as vital, experimental micro-ecologies where everyday people learn to administer their own networks free from corporate surveillance.

A sharp critic will immediately land on the hardest objection to this techno-socialist horizon: How can independent cooperatives and open-source networks possibly survive against Big Tech monopolies? As I acknowledged above, capitalist mega-platforms like Amazon or Google can utilize their vast cloud rent to operate at massive financial losses, utilizing predatory pricing to crush rivals while retaining the gatekeeping power to arbitrarily block independent apps from their mobile stores.

This is precisely why we cannot rely on ethical consumerism alone. While everyday people choosing civic values over individual convenience is the ideal, history shows we cannot lay the burden of structural change solely on the consumer. To overcome Big Tech’s gatekeeping lock-in, democratic governments must aggressively intervene. We must pass uncompromising regulations that force corporate platforms to open their borders, mandate strict algorithmic transparency, and deploy public capital to fund digital commons as essential public infrastructure.

Part VI: Monday Morning and the Massive Data Strike

Ultimately, human beings only act collectively when they face a clearly defined common enemy. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the threat of a global virus united the international scientific community in unprecedented ways. In our current era of technofeudalism, our common enemy is the profound, unchecked asymmetry of power held by the billionaire-monarchs of Big Tech. They are the direct antagonists of global democracy.

What, then, does the concrete revolution look like for a normal person who finishes reading this essay and asks: "Okay, what do I literally do on Monday morning?"

The first step is a personal refusal of digital serfdom. When you wake up on Monday morning, you must begin to audit your own digital dependency. You must ask yourself why we continue to tolerate an unelected corporate "government-company" to decide our future and stand above planetary-scale laws. Instead of yielding to convenience, the first step is to start switching to open platforms, exploring decentralised networks, and moving away from costly, proprietary corporate ecosystems. It is a commitment to a new day where education and equal rights are the foundational keys to social harmony.

The second, more radical step is a collective transition from individual opting-out to a massive, coordinated data strike. Because platforms are functionally worthless without the continuous attention, free labour, and data streams of their users, we hold the ultimate economic leverage. If tomorrow, half of the global user base coordinated to suspend their apps, refuse tracking cookies, and delete their stored archives, the impact on cloud capital would be instantaneous and catastrophic. For a platform like Meta, which commands over 3.6 billion daily active users, a mass data strike would trigger an unpredictable collapse in share value, echoing the devastating global economic shocks of the Wall Street Crash of 1929 or the Great Recession of 2008. By starving these platforms of their vital raw materials, we can force the collapse of algorithmic governmentality from below.

With this essay, I aim to show that the architecture of our digital future is not predetermined. While the current trajectory points toward a deeply unequal society managed by extractive monopolies, the transition toward techno-socialism is an active, ongoing struggle. Different types of resistance through digital workerism and the push for a massive data labour movement show that platform workers and internet users are organising to demand algorithmic transparency, fair labour conditions, and property rights over the value their digital interactions create.

The crisis of platform capitalism is fundamentally a crisis of ownership. It is an exploitative system designed to make cloudalists richer by using us as free labour while keeping us perpetually distracted so we do not see or understand the problem. Because current mega-platforms already function as proto-states and essential infrastructure, we must begin treating them as true public goods. By embedding democratic governance, equity, and social solidarity directly into the code and ownership structures of our platforms, we can successfully reverse the re-feudalisation of the internet and reclaim our technological land.
