## Wenqian Huang
# From Cloud to Cloud
**The Flâneur, Reflective Architecture, and the Circulation of Urban Images**

Glass in the city sometimes makes people stop.  
   
While walking through the city, glass curtain walls often bring the sky, streets, vehicles, pedestrians and surrounding buildings onto the same surface. Things that belong to different distances and spatial layers are compressed by reflection into a temporary flat image.  
   
These reflections are usually unstable. They change with light, weather, viewing angle and the position of the passer-by. When the viewer moves forward or steps back, the image on the building surface also changes. In this sense, the reflective facade is not only an exterior material of architecture. It also takes part in organising how people look at the city.  
   
This article follows what happens after such a reflected image appears. A cloud in the sky is first turned by the glass facade into an optical image. It may then be photographed by a smartphone, become a digital file, and finally enter cloud storage and networked platforms. In this process, computational media do not simply preserve the architectural reflection. They extend, modify and redistribute it.  
   
The reflection, therefore, does not end on the glass surface. It moves from the physical city into computational systems, continuing to circulate across devices, files and platforms.  
   
This kind of pause changes the act of walking. Moving through the city is no longer only a movement from one place to another. The viewer is interrupted by the image on the building surface and begins to adjust their position according to light, angle and reflection. A clear route is briefly disturbed, and another kind of route begins to appear: not one drawn by urban planning, but one organised by the facade, the viewer’s position and the temporary image that forms between them.  
   
Walter Benjamin’s The Arcades Project offers a way to think about this kind of urban attention. Benjamin writes about the arcades of nineteenth-century Paris, where glass, iron, shop windows, commodities, crowds and artificial light formed a modern space between the street and the interior. The arcade was not simply a passage for movement, nor was it an ordinary room. Bodies passed through it; gazes moved across it; commodities were displayed; desire was organised; and the city became readable through fragments.  
   
The figure of the flâneur is closely connected to this space. The flâneur is often understood as an urban wanderer, but this figure is not simply someone who walks around the city. The flâneur reads the city through pauses, turns and accidental attention. He does not begin from a total plan of the city, but from shop windows, mirrors, signs, light, crowds and surfaces. Wandering is not a lack of direction. It is a way of allowing what is encountered on the way to redirect attention.  
   
If this way of looking is brought into the contemporary city, glass remains important, but its role has changed. In the arcade, glass framed commodities and mediated the relation between street and interior. In contemporary reflective facades, glass often hides the inside of the building and sends the outside world back to the viewer. Instead of seeing what happens inside the building, one sees the sky, roads, cars and one’s own body spread across its surface.  
   
The reflective facade can therefore be understood as another kind of passage. It may not allow the body to pass through, but it allows light, urban space and image to move between different states. It is not simply a wall, nor only a mirror. It is an intermediate surface where reality begins to take the form of an image.  
   
In this sense, architecture comes before the camera.  
   
A reflective facade does not neutrally copy the outside world. The image it forms depends on the orientation of the building, the colour of the glass, the curve of the surface, the weather, the light and the viewer’s position. Flat glass may divide the sky into a grid. A curved facade may distort the shape of nearby buildings. Dark glass may alter the brightness of clouds and streets. If the viewer moves only a little, the spatial relation inside the reflection also changes.  
   
The building is not merely an object inside a photograph. It has already produced an image before photography takes place. It cuts the sky, compresses distance, and places distant buildings, nearby cars, moving clouds and the body of the passer-by onto the same surface. The photographer does not face an untouched reality, but an image already organised by architecture, light and the city.  
   
This also changes the position of the photographer. The photographer is not always an author first. They may first be a viewer.  
   
The discussion of “audience photography” in Mirroring（te magazine Nº3，2024）offers a useful way to understand this. Photographing does not always begin with a fully active author. Sometimes the photographer is first turned into a viewer by a work, a space or a visual relation, and only afterwards participates through the act of taking a photograph. Photography does not start from an empty position. It continues a relation of looking that has already begun.  
   
Photography of reflective facades works in a similar way. The urban wanderer is first drawn by the building surface. They stop, adjust their position, and observe how the reflection changes with the movement of the body. Only then is the phone lifted. The smartphone is not the starting point of the whole image process. It is the second transformation of an image already produced by the building.  
   
This is one difference between reflective architecture and an ordinary urban view. In front of a normal wall, the viewer usually remains outside the architectural surface. In front of reflective glass, the viewer has already entered the image. Body, cloud, cars, trees and distant buildings appear together on the facade. The viewer looks at the city, but also becomes part of the city image.  
   
This may help explain why reflective architecture often invites photography. A cloud in the sky is open and without a frame. It does not easily form a stable composition with the human body. The curtain wall gives it scale, division and position. Architecture seems to have done part of the photographic work in advance, turning a continuous and changing reality into an image waiting to be captured.  
   
Yet the phone does not simply keep this image as it is. When the reflection is photographed, light enters the sensor and is converted into electronic signals. The phone processes exposure, colour, contrast and dynamic range. The image may be brightened, sharpened, compressed, encoded and attached to information such as time, location and device data. What appears on the screen may look like a record of what has just been seen, but it is already another version.  
   
Architecture works with light and space.The phone works with signals and data. From glass to phone, the image shifts from an optical image to a computational image. The computation does not happen on the glass itself. It happens when the reflection is captured by the sensor, processed by software, encoded as a file and given metadata. A reflection that once depended on weather, angle and bodily presence becomes a digital object that can be copied, searched, edited and transmitted.  
   
After the wanderer’s body leaves the site, the image can still continue to move. This is the process of moving from cloud to cloud.  
   
The first cloud is a meteorological event in the sky. It is formed by water vapour, temperature and air movement. It changes continuously and moves with the weather. It can only be seen from a particular place and at a particular moment; it cannot truly be possessed.  
   
The second cloud is the cloud of computational systems. It is not weather, but a structure of storage and access made of servers, data centres, cables, accounts, synchronisation systems and interfaces. It is called a cloud, as if it were light and immaterial, but it depends on very material infrastructures.  
   
Between these two clouds, the image passes through a chain of transformations: the cloud in the sky becomes an optical reflection on glass; the reflection is captured by a phone; the phone turns it into a computational image; the image is encoded as a file; the file enters the data cloud and continues to circulate across devices and platforms.  
   
The first cloud reaches architecture as light. The second cloud receives it as data.  
   
This is not a simple jump from “reality” to “the virtual”. The image does not suddenly leave the physical world and enter a space without material conditions. It passes through architecture, device, account, address, interface and user, being registered, converted, stored and redistributed step by step. Benjamin’s arcade helps us understand how bodies moved through the glass spaces of the modern city. In the contemporary computational city, images also move through a system made of city, cloud, interface, address and user. Bratton’s account of layered computation is useful here not as an external theoretical frame, but as a way to see that once an image on a reflective facade is photographed, it no longer belongs only to the building site. It enters a continuous environment made by both urban space and computational space.  
   
The reflective facade is therefore not outside the computational system. It is one of the surfaces through which the image begins to enter it. The facade organises reality into an optical image ready to be captured, while the phone and the cloud give that image a form that can be stored, synchronised and called up again.  
   
Once the image enters the data cloud, it does not stop. It may be synchronised across several devices, placed in a digital album, attached to a time and location, uploaded to a social platform, or later arranged by the system as a “memory”. It may also be cropped, compressed, edited, forwarded, screenshotted and shown on someone else’s screen.  
   
At this point, the movement of the image is no longer decided by weather, sunlight or viewing angle, but by file systems, platform interfaces, search, recommendation and archiving. The original reflection may have lasted only a few seconds. The digital image can reappear after the weather has changed and after the photographer has left the site.  
   
This may be understood as the image’s second wandering. The first wandering belongs to the body. The flâneur moves through the city, stops in front of the building and finds the reflection.  
   
The second wandering belongs to the image. It moves from glass to phone, from phone to file, from file to cloud and platform, and finally to the interfaces of other viewers.  
   
These two forms of wandering are not separate. The body’s pause allows the image to be captured, while the later circulation of the image records where the body stopped, when it looked, what device it used, and how the image was stored or shared. While the wanderer records the city, the computational system also records the wanderer.  
   
This marks an important difference between today’s wanderer and Benjamin’s flâneur. The nineteenth-century flâneur read modern life through the surfaces of the city. The contemporary wanderer also turns these surfaces into data. They are at once viewer and image producer; they build a personal visual archive while leaving traces of time, location and behaviour within platform systems. Authorship therefore becomes difficult to locate.  
   
The building first forms the image. The viewer chooses a position. The phone processes the light. Software produces the file. The cloud stores the image. The platform arranges how it appears. Algorithms affect when, at what size and to whom it is shown. The making and circulation of the image are distributed across several actors and technical processes.  
   
Architecture is the first producer of the image. The photographer is both viewer and participant. The phone carries out a second production. The cloud and platform take part in later storage, editing and distribution. This distributed relation also changes what it means to have seen something.  
   
When a reflected image has passed through architecture, camera, software, file, cloud and platform, seeing is no longer a direct relation between the human eye and reality. The viewer first sees a city reorganised by the building. The phone then processes it as a digital image. Metadata gives it time and place. The cloud extends its duration. The platform gives it a new context for being seen.  
   
What people later trust may not only be their memory of the site, but also the file, coordinates and timestamp recorded by the device. A photograph seems to prove that the moment happened. Yet what it proves is not an unmediated reality, but an image path that has passed through several surfaces and systems.  
   
This does not mean that visual authority has completely moved from humans to machines. The viewer still decides where to stop, what to photograph, how to frame it, and whether to keep or share the image. But from the moment the shutter is pressed, the image’s mode of existence is no longer controlled by the viewer alone. It is shaped by architecture, sensor, software, cloud and platform together.  
   
Seeing is not simply invalidated, nor is it fully handed over to the device. It is redistributed. Visual trust is formed between the eye, the glass, the camera, the file, the metadata, the cloud and the interface.  
   
This is why the reflective facade needs to be understood again. It is not merely a background for photography, nor only an architectural surface that resembles a screen. It is closer to a first turning point through which urban images enter computational systems. It allows sky, architecture, body and street to become an optical image, and then allows that image to move into devices, files and clouds.  
   
From cloud to cloud is not a simple route of preservation. It is a media chain in which the image changes its state, its authorship and its conditions of being seen. The flâneur moves through the city. The image moves through architecture, device and computational system.  
   
What continues to exist in the second cloud is not a simple copy of the first cloud. It is another version produced by nature, architecture, photography and computation together. In this version, a contemporary way of seeing the city becomes clearer: people still walk through the street, but images have learned to keep wandering through systems.
