When the rain hits the ground, it passes through a web of roots and soil. Some of it finds its way into river channels, contributing to the flow, eventually reaching the sea, evaporating, and returning as rain clouds. This self-sustaining loop has been running for more than four billion years, and has been home to countless different life forms. Human intervention has broken that loop.

I am here at Westow Park. A well-managed park greets me. It is clearly quite hilly, unlike most of London's terrain. Following the steep grassy ground lined with big trees, it links to Upper Norwood Recreation Ground. Unlike Westow Park, this area has fewer trees but wide open grass hills. I keep walking down the hill, the park on my left and residential blocks on my right. Birds are chirping, and the fresh smell of rain surrounds me. This is said to be where the river Effra once flowed through, and the upstream stretch still acts as a water source for the buried river.

Passing the park, I turn right to keep following the former river's course. A big hill rises ahead, then drops down to the Church of the Faithful Virgin. This church was known to have been flooded by the Effra repeatedly, and there are supposedly marks left on the outside brick wall. Though I couldn't find any visible trace of them. The path to the church took far longer than it should have. I kept getting lost until I realised I should just head downhill. After a few times of going up and down, my walk connects to Norwood Park. The terrain remains hilly and green throughout. In this park, you can still reportedly see open water when it's wet enough. The river trees, willows and alders are quietly signalling the river's presence, while showing humanity's attempt to modify the land around it. These trees are known as *pioneer trees* which quickly grow back to damaged lands (Newman*,* 2016*)*. Especially, the alder trees form a symbiosis relationship with Frankia bacteria living in the root nodules, which fix nitrogen and neutralise the soil (Mallet and Roy, 2014). Anna Tsing (2015) argues that there are other ways of making worlds, that collaborative survival requires cross-species coordination. Twenty of the thirty-eight tributaries that once flowed into the Thames are now culverted and erased from the surface, repurposed as sewers (Thames21, 2023). These waterways disappeared from sight, yet continued to persist.

To find the traces of these hidden rivers and re-map them,I am walking the former river beds. The River Effra, culverted in the mid-nineteenth century, is my primary route.

On foot, the river makes itself known differently. Green areas give way to flatter ground, the terrain quietly levelling where water once ran. The residential streets continue, but something in the landscape has shifted. The river's former presence is now absorbed into the geometry of infrastructure beneath.

Near the cemetery, a stink pipe catches my attention. Installed to vent gases from the Effra flowing below, it rises from the pavement as a thin steel column, easy to miss. The cemetery itself was acquired in 1836, while the Effra still ran openly through it. In Victorian times, a coffin was discovered floating in the Thames and was found to have come from the cemetery (Talling, no date). Now both lie underneath, the river and dead together, vented by the same pipe.

This pleasant walk continues through the beautiful neighborhood of Dulwich. This area has a large amount of trees and green areas. In Belair Park, there is a man-made lake made by damming the Effra when it was a still clean, flowing river in the 1780s (Southwark Council, no date). Decades later, when the Effra began smelling and polluted, being culverted underground as a sewer, the lake remained at the site. Now, the lake is surviving as an urban nature loved by locals and the animals.

As the walk continues to Brixton, I realise the trace of Effra is fading. The roads are laid straight with asphalt, more less nature, heavily developed city, between them, only few young trees planted along the narrow strip of grass area between roads and estates where the Effra used to flow remark.

As the walk continues into Brixton, the traces of the Effra become harder to read. Roads run straight and asphalt, the neighbourhood heavily developed, nature compressed into narrow grass strips between roads and estates, thin lines that mark where the river once flowed. The river has not disappeared so much as been translated: its course absorbed into the logic of the city's layout, its presence redistributed across infrastructure. Bruno Latour (2005) suggests that non-human actants continue to shape social worlds even when they recede from view. The Effra is still here, in the gradient of the land, in the alignment of streets, in the strip of soil narrow enough to overlook.

## 51°29'14.3"N 0°07'33.1"W

Vauxhall outfall: Brixton and Clapham Storm Relief CSO

The walk ends where the Effra meets the Thames, at Vauxhall. What was once a river mouth is now a storm discharge point—the same culverted channel that carries the Effra now releases sewage overflow when rain or tidal pressure exceeds the system's capacity. The infrastructure here has its own history: Joseph Bazalgette's sewer network, funded in the aftermath of the Great Stink of 1858, was the engineering response to a river system already beyond repair. Thames Water's average spill duration runs longer than the English water company mean, and between 2019 and 2023, foul sewers were among the greatest sources of pollution incidents recorded in London (London Assembly Research Unit, 2025). The river persists here too, not as water, but as data, as infrastructure under pressure.

In February 2025, the Tideway tunnel began operating, London's new super sewer running beneath the Thames. The Effra's former mouth was also renovated as public space during the Tideway's construction. Concrete and engineered riverbank now mark the site. The intervention is vast, technically ambitious, and entirely human-centred in its logic. The river it manages has no say in the matter.

Just about 1 km to the east upstream from where Brixton and Clapham Storm Relief is, I could locate another storm discharge point from Thames Water. Interestingly, Thames Water has been discharging sewage into the river many times since May to June. There is no direct evidence linking these discharges to the Effra specifically, but the proximity makes the connection difficult to ignore.

## 51°28'59.3"N 0°10'02.3"W

Cadogan Pier: Water quality sensor

Standing at the outfall, I begin to wonder what actually happens to the river when overflow occurs. Does it only happen when it rains? What does it do to the water?

To look further, I access open data from the Environment Agency's API, retrieving water quality readings from the sensor at Cadogan Pier: Dissolved Oxygen, Temperature, Salinity, Ammonium, pH, and Conductivity. One day after a recorded discharge event at South West Storm Relief (2026-06-02T04:26:00), the data shows a drop in dissolved oxygen. Because the Thames is tidal, water moves in and out with temporal delay—discharge happens, and its chemical effects propagate through the system unevenly, in layers. The connection between the storm discharge and the oxygen drop is not scientifically proven here. I did not have enough data to generalise, nor sufficient time to research water quality in depth.

What I am attempting is something different. Gabrys and Pritchard (2018) propose that sensing need not be understood as a human mind decoding an external world, but as experience expressed through subjects in relation, a collaborative undertaking between entities. This collaborative sensing is what I am reaching toward with the API data: not extracting information about the river, but entering into the network of relations the river is still part of.

Where could the coordinates of humanity be?

I walked the Effra from upstream to downstream, collecting body data, taking photographs, feeling and trying to understand. As the river neared the Thames, the visible traces gave way to something denser: infrastructure, ecological entanglement, data.

And yet the river is still here. Despite being buried and absorbed into the sewer system, the Effra continues to act as collective urban infrastructure. Citizens are organising to clean the rivers. Water companies are being pressured for transparency on sewage discharge. Even the stink pipes, the organic growth along drainage channels, the narrow grass strips between roads matter. The willow trees that grew back into land that was stripped and sealed over now offer shade to people who do not know what flows beneath them. The overflow that escapes the system on a rainy weekend can reach a swimmer by Monday. The river moves through all of it.

Jane Bennett (2010) argues that recognising agency as distributed—not solely the province of humans—might cultivate a more ecologically responsible politics: one less concerned with blaming individuals than with tracing the web of forces shaping situations and events. By taking the river, an ordinarily overlooked object, as a subject rather than a backdrop, it becomes possible to make visible political and ecological questions that were previously unasked.

Where, then, are the coordinates of humanity in this system? The human attempt to control and modify every being that is not human did not simply make us a dominant species; it made us part of the system. Observed with care and interest, the river emerges with larger questions than infrastructure alone.
